Understanding the Job Automation Risk Calculator

This notebook walks through the hazard model that powers the calculator so you can interpret the curves, pressure-test scenarios, and translate the output into career choices.

Updated October 2025

What the calculator actually simulates

The experience you see on the main page sits on top of a stacked hazard model. Each year carries some probability that your role is displaced; integrate those annual hazards and you get the cumulative chance that automation has arrived. The blue curve on the chart is the survival complement for technical feasibility, and it comes straight from that integration.

Survival relationship
$$P_{\text{loss}}(t) = 1 - \exp\left(-\int_0^t \lambda_{\text{total}}(s)\, ds\right)$$

In this release the interactive focuses on the AI hazard term. We treat macro shocks, firm strategy, and personal agency as separate layers, but the simulator you are using keeps the spotlight on how quickly AI can credibly do the work and how much friction stands in its way.

AI hazard core
$$\lambda_{\text{AI}}(s) = \min\!\left[0.95,\; h_{\text{base}}(s)\, \exp\Bigg(\sum w_{\text{amp}} x_i\Bigg)\, \exp\Bigg(\sum w_{\text{fric}} x_i\Bigg)\right]$$

The base hazard is a three-phase curve. When capability is \(<10\%\) of the threshold we pin hazard at 0.1%. It ramps linearly as AI closes the gap, and once capability exceeds the threshold we track $$h_{\text{base}}(s) = h_0 \left(\frac{A(s)}{\theta_0}\right)^{\gamma}$$ then damp it by organizational friction and seniority. A few levers matter inside that term:

  • Capability growth $A(t)$: the AI doubling slider sets $$A(t)=\exp\!\left(\frac{24 \ln 2}{\text{doubling months}} \, t\right)$$ so shorter doubling times bend the blue curve forward.
  • Compute scaling: capability is multiplied by $(\text{multiplier})^{t}$ each year to reflect compute-led efficiency gains.
  • Industry pace: a constant multiplier captures diffusion pressure from your sector.
  • Friction term $H(t)$: answers initialize $H(0)$ and it decays as $$H(t) = H(0)\, e^{-0.02 t}$$ so stubborn organizations eventually soften.
  • Threshold $\theta_0$: we start at $3.0$ for entry roles and scale it by $[1.00, 1.61, 2.00, 2.11, 2.43]$ as seniority climbs, forcing higher capability before leaders are threatened.
  • Seniority damper: once above threshold we multiply by $(1 - r_s)$ where $r_s \in \{0, 0.45, 0.65, 0.85, 0.95\}$ from entry through executive to reflect retention bias.
  • Friction multiplier: the hazard is further reduced by $$\frac{1}{1 + \kappa \tfrac{H(t)}{5}}$$ with $\kappa = 0.3$, so persistent friction buys time.

We run Simpson's rule over that hazard curve to deliver the technical-feasibility probability you see in the chart.

How your answers move the curve

Every prompt in the wizard maps to a coded value $x_i$. Likert responses convert to the symmetric set $\{-2,-1,0,+1,+2\}$, while yes or no toggles land at $\pm1$. The model tracks two channels: an amplifier sum $\sum w_{\text{amp}} x_i$ and a friction sum $\sum w_{\text{fric}} x_i$. After clamping each sum to $[-2, 2]$ we exponentiate them, so the combined multiplier ranges from about $0.14\times$ to $7.4\times$ the baseline hazard.

The prompts cluster into four themes:

  • AI readiness (Q1–Q4): high scores strengthen the amplifier channel when you report top-tier models, abundant data, crisp benchmarks, and digitized workflows.
  • Task structure (Q5–Q9): complex decomposition and tacit knowledge feed the protective friction channel, while rapid feedback loops and standardization accelerate adoption.
  • Human moat (Q10–Q13): relationship intensity, unions, physical presence, and trust demands all load the friction side and slow the curve.
  • Firm and personal context (Q14–Q21): company levers shape implementation delay, and adaptability questions (Q18, Q20) drive re-employment probability rather than the hazard directly.

We also tweak the gate steepness $\gamma$ via a lighter-weight pass inside kEff. Structured work, fast feedback, and standardized tasks sharpen the S-curve; messy, collaborative, or bespoke work flattens it. That captures whether adoption snaps into place once the threshold is crossed or grinds along for years.

Calibration check: every page load reruns unit tests. Pushing Q1, Q4, and Q6 to five yields roughly a $2.5\times$ hazard boost versus neutral answers, while stacking protective responses (Q5, Q7, Q8, Q16 high) suppresses the hazard by about $50\%$. If a future edit drifts, the console will flag it.

Implementation delay and the green curve

The green actual job-loss curve is the same hazard shifted forward by the implementation delay we derive from company context and individual leverage. Q14 through Q17 look at adoption appetite, labor cost pressure, labor-market tightness, and infrastructure. Q19 and Q21 reward complementary work and strong performance.

Delay function
$$\text{delay} = \big(1.75 - 1.25\, s_{\text{delay}}\big)\, m_{\text{seniority}}, \quad s_{\text{delay}} \in [-2,2],\; m_{\text{seniority}} \in \{0.8,1.2,1.4,1.6,1.8\}$$

Positive scores mean less delay: an early-adopting firm with high wage pressure can collapse the gap toward $0.5$ years, while defensive environments stretch it beyond three. Senior leaders get another bump because their multiplier reaches $1.8\times$.

Re-employment odds pull from the adaptability prompts. We start at $60\%$, add or subtract up to $20$ points based on transferable skills and learning speed, subtract up to $30$ points if the median technical hazard lands within three years, and then apply a small seniority boost (up to $12\%$). The result is clamped between $10\%$ and $85\%$ — nobody is guaranteed, but nobody is written off.

Reading the outputs

The chart shows two survival complements: blue for technical feasibility and green for realized displacement once friction and delay kick in. Because we integrate $\lambda(s)$ with Simpson's rule, the area under each curve is the cumulative probability that automation has already happened.

  • Median timeline: we binary-search for the year where cumulative probability hits $50\%$. That becomes the headline median job-loss metric.
  • Risk by 2030 or 2031: the model evaluates $P_{\text{loss}}$ at $5.35$ and $6.35$ years from $2024.65$, matching the scenario years shown on the card.
  • Personal timeline: we surface the $25\%$, $50\%$, $75\%$, and $90\%$ milestones when they fall within the next $10$, $15$, $18$, and $25$ years respectively so you can see the near-term thresholds.
  • Re-employment label: the probability is bucketed into Good, Moderate, Low, or Very Low for quick scanning, with the precise percentage alongside.

Use the growth sliders to stress-test assumptions. Faster AI doubling or heavier compute scaling pivots both curves left. Increasing friction answers or dialing up implementation drag leaves the blue curve anchored but pushes the green curve out, telling you whether you are dealing with imminent technical substitution or a slower organizational grind.

How to work with the model

The calculator is most useful when you treat it like a scenario notebook rather than an oracle. A few patterns from user research help:

  1. Start with the preset that feels closest to your role, then tweak one cluster of questions at a time so you can see which dimensions move the hazard.
  2. Stress-test the extremes: set AI readiness to five while leaving friction high, then flip it. The distance between those runs is a map of where to invest your energy.
  3. Pair the re-employment score with the green curve. A fast hazard plus weak re-employment is the trigger for up-skilling or a job search; a slow hazard plus strong re-employment buys time for deliberate moves.

Remember that this model isolates the AI capability channel. Macro cycles, regulation, leadership changes, and your own agency remain outside the frame. Use the weight structure as a checklist: strengthen the protective items you can influence, document the assumptions you cannot, and revisit the calculator as real-world signals shift.
