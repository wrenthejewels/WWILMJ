Model Guide

Let's walk through the hazard model that powers the calculator so you can interpret the curves, pressure-test scenarios, and translate the output into career choices.

Updated October 2025
METR Measuring AI ability to complete long tasks

METR's March 2025 study tracks how long a generalist frontier agent can work autonomously before its success rate drops below a chosen threshold. Their task horizon has doubled roughly every seven months since 2019, giving us a grounded baseline rather than guessing at capability curves.

The calculator loads the latest SOTA (state-of-the-art) model benchmarks from benchmark_results.yaml on each page load, using the model's release date as the capability baseline and the current date (when you access the page) as the starting point for projections. For example, GPT-5's benchmark shows a 50th percentile task horizon of 137 minutes and an 80% success horizon of 26.37 minutes. We fit a logistic success curve through those quantiles so the reliability adjustment becomes $f(r) = \exp(-\sigma\,\operatorname{logit}(r))$ with $\sigma \approx -1.19$. At the default 95% production bar the effective horizon contracts to roughly 4.1 minutes (about 33× shorter than the 50th percentile), and a 99% requirement would shrink it to about 0.58 minutes (~235× shorter). As METR publishes new benchmarks, simply update the YAML file to keep projections current.

  • Doubling trend: METR's doubling time drives how fast AI capability improves. Every 7 months, AI can handle 2x longer tasks at the same success rate.
  • Reliability gate: We require 95% success before automation kicks in. Technical capability requires a high bar to outperform human error, though the calculator allows a custom reliability gate if tasks can feasibly be automated at a lower success rate.
  • Task mapping: Your job gets decomposed into five task duration categories borrowed from METR's framework (<5 min, 5-15 min, 15-60 min, 1-3 hr, >9 hr), but with gate thresholds recalibrated for corporate production work: [2.5, 15, 60, 180, 540] minutes. These longer thresholds reflect typical workplace task decomposition, which runs longer than METR's research-oriented benchmarks. Once AI can reliably complete enough of these categories, the hazard channel opens.
  • Scenario levers: Industry-specific friction and required reliability, plus your questionnaire responses, adjust capability but never overwrite METR's doubling - the 7-month doubling stays fixed. Friction multiplies the horizon penalty (p_domain); reliability tightens the success bar.

What the calculator actually simulates

The calculator models job displacement as a survival problem where multiple hazard channels compound over time. A true stacked hazard model should consider all other possibilities for displacement, including macroeconomic forces, firm-level performance, reductions in force (RIFs), non-AI displacement hazards, and personal leverage. This calculator focuses only on AI-related hazards at the role, personal, and firm levels. Consider that it is only a portion of your total displacement risk. This model will update over time with new AI-related data and risk considerations.

Survival relationship
$$P_{\text{loss}}(t) = 1 - \exp\left(-\int_0^t \lambda_{\text{total}}(s)\, ds\right)$$
Stacked hazard model
$$\lambda_{\text{total}}(s) = \lambda_{\text{AI}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{macro}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{firm}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{role}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{personal}}(s)$$

The active AI hazard channel ($\lambda_{\text{AI}}(s)$) generates the blue technical feasibility curve. Your firm and personal questionnaire responses then shift this curve forward in time to create the green implementation curve. The same data also determines your re-employment probability.

  • AI hazard: METR task-length data map capability progress into the blue curve.
  • Adaptability delay: Firm and personal answers shift the AI hazard forward in time to produce the green curve.
  • Re-employment likelihood: Adaptability and transferable skill questions translate into the percentage you see on the dashboard.

Parameter Glossary

  • t: Time in years from present (when you access the calculator)
  • H_r(t): AI capability — maximum task length in minutes AI can complete at reliability r; higher values = more capable AI
  • A(t): AI Readiness ∈ [0,1] — fraction of your job tasks AI can perform (0 = can't do any tasks, 1 = can do all tasks)
  • D: METR doubling period (7 months, empirically grounded, fixed — never modified by questions or sliders)
  • f(r): Reliability adjustment factor (r=0.95 default requires ~33× capability margin; r=0.99 requires ~235× margin)
  • L_i: Task duration thresholds for each bucket gate (2.5, 15, 60, 180, 540 minutes)
  • w_i: Task bucket weights — personalized based on your role, sum to 1.0 (e.g., 25%, 35%, 25%, 10%, 5% baseline)
  • s: Logistic softness parameter (0.45 default) — controls how gradually task gates open as AI capability increases
  • h_AI: Maximum base annual hazard (0.40 = 40% per year displacement risk at full automation)
  • γ: Hazard gate steepness (8.0 default) — controls S-curve sharpness (how quickly risk ramps up once threshold is crossed)
  • θ: Coverage threshold — what fraction of job tasks must be automatable before hazard activates (baseline 0.50, adjusted by role characteristics)
  • userMult: Question-based hazard multiplier, bounded [0.33×, 3.0×] for model responsiveness

Friction Decay Parameter

The model includes an inferred friction decay rate (λ) that captures how quickly organizational and role-specific barriers erode over time. This parameter is calculated from your questionnaire responses about company adoption readiness, infrastructure, labor market dynamics, and role characteristics:

Friction decay rate
$$\lambda = 0.02 + 0.01 \times (n_{12} - 0.5) + 0.01 \times (n_{15} - 0.5) + 0.005 \times (n_{13} - 0.5) + 0.005 \times (n_{14} - 0.5) - 0.01 \times (n_{11} - 0.5) - 0.01 \times (n_{10} - 0.5)$$

where $n_i$ is the normalized answer to question $i$ (Likert 1-5 scaled to 0-1), and $\lambda$ is clamped to $[0.005, 0.05]$

  • Q12 (Company AI adoption): +0.01 weight — more adoption-ready organizations have faster friction decay
  • Q15 (IT infrastructure): +0.01 weight — modern infrastructure accelerates integration
  • Q13 (Labor cost pressure): +0.005 weight — cost sensitivity speeds up automation adoption
  • Q14 (Labor market tightness): +0.005 weight — hiring difficulty encourages automation alternatives
  • Q11 (Physical presence): -0.01 weight — physical requirements slow organizational change
  • Q10 (Human judgment): -0.01 weight — relationship-intensive work resists automation longer

This parameter influences how quickly implementation barriers diminish as AI capability continues to grow. The model implements dynamic friction decay where the implementation delay decreases exponentially over time: $\Delta(t) = \Delta_0 \cdot e^{-\lambda t}$. At time $t$, the green curve (actual job loss) is calculated as the technical feasibility at the earlier time $t - \Delta(t)$. Higher λ means organizational friction erodes faster (barriers collapse quickly once AI crosses initial thresholds); lower λ means barriers persist longer even as technical capability advances. This captures how, for example, regulatory frameworks and organizational inertia may initially delay adoption by several years, but these delays shrink as AI becomes more capable and widespread adoption creates network effects.

Role Presets

Different roles face different automation barriers. Software engineering maps most directly to AI's training data and requires less physical presence, so it gets lower friction (1.05×). Legal work and traditional engineering involve more tacit knowledge, regulatory constraints, and liability concerns, earning higher friction multipliers (1.45-1.50×). These conservative priors reflect how far capability must stretch beyond METR's software-centric benchmarks to reach production readiness in each domain.

Two-layer friction system: The model applies friction at both industry and role levels. The industry-specific friction slider affects all roles in a sector (e.g., healthcare 1.5× applies to doctors, nurses, and administrators). Your questionnaire responses then add role-specific friction based on tacit knowledge (Q9), physical presence (Q11), and context dependencies (Q7). Both layers multiply together in the calculation, so a high-context legal role in a regulated industry faces compounded barriers.

  • Domain friction (preset baseline): Role presets set an initial friction multiplier (software 1.05×, admin/data 1.10×, finance/consulting 1.35×, legal 1.45×, engineering 1.50×). This serves as the starting point for the role's automation difficulty before individual job characteristics are considered.
  • Recommended reliability: Presets suggest a reliability starting point per role based on typical error tolerance and stakes (finance/legal 97-98%, software/data 92-93%, creative/customer service 88-90%). Seniority adjusts these baselines: entry-level roles tolerate slightly lower reliability (-0.05), while executive decisions demand higher confidence (+0.05). Higher reliability requirements contract the effective task horizon significantly—95% reliability shrinks capability by ~33×, while 98% shrinks it by ~95×. You can override both friction and reliability in the UI to test different scenarios.

In addition to the preset baseline, the calculator derives a role-specific domain alignment penalty from your answers. Digitized, decomposable, and standardized work (Q4/Q5/Q6 high) reduces the penalty; context dependence, tacit knowledge, judgment/relationship load, and physical presence (Q7/Q9/Q10/Q11 high) increase it. Internally, we form a signed weighted sum and map it to a penalty multiplier via an exponential, clamped to a reasonable range. This penalty multiplies with the sector slider, so misalignment compounds friction.

AI hazard core
$$\lambda_{\text{AI}}(s) = \frac{h_{\max}}{1 + \exp\!\big(-\gamma (A_{\text{job}}(s) - \theta)\big)} \cdot M_{\text{user}}(s)$$

The hazard turns on once METR readiness clears the coverage bar. The model uses a two-level gating system: first, task-level gates determine which individual tasks AI can handle; second, a job-level hazard gate opens when enough of your job becomes automatable. Here's how it works:

  1. Task-level gates open gradually: As AI capability $H_r(t)$ grows via METR's doubling trend, each task bucket's gate $G_i(H)$ opens. Short tasks automate first (gates open when $H$ exceeds their threshold $L_i$), then medium tasks, then long tasks. Your personalized weights $w_i$ determine how much each gate contributes to overall readiness.
  2. Job readiness accumulates: $A(t) = \sum w_i G_i(H)$ sums up all the open gates, weighted by your job profile. This gives a number between 0 and 1 representing what fraction of your job AI can perform.
  3. Job-level hazard gate activates: When $A(t)$ crosses the coverage threshold $\theta$ (typically 0.50), the hazard function $\lambda_{\text{AI}}(t)$ rapidly increases via the logistic gate. Before this point, hazard is near zero; after, it approaches the maximum $h_{\max} \times M_{\text{user}}$.
Task horizon
$$H_r(t) = \frac{H_{50,0} \cdot 2^{(t \cdot 12)/D}}{f(r)}$$

This structure means automation doesn't happen all at once. AI steadily masters more of your tasks (task gates opening), but job displacement risk only spikes once AI can handle enough of your role to justify replacement (job gate opening). Jobs with more long-duration tasks take longer to reach this threshold.

  • Task horizon $H_r(t)$: $$H_r(t) = \frac{H_{50,0} \cdot 2^{(t \cdot 12)/D}}{f(r)}$$ where $D = 7$ months is METR's doubling period (fixed). $f(r)$ is the reliability factor: higher reliability requirements divide $H$, reducing AI's effective maximum task length. At the default 95% reliability, $f(0.95) \approx 33.4$, contracting the 50th percentile horizon (137 min) down to about 4.1 minutes for production use.
  • Personalized task weights $w_i$: Your answers to Q5 (task decomposability), Q6 (task standardization), Q7 (context dependency), and your seniority level determine how your job breaks down across the five buckets. Entry-level roles skew toward shorter tasks (quick emails, simple coding); executive roles skew toward longer tasks (strategic planning, integration work). Highly structured jobs (Q5=5) concentrate weight in the short buckets; complex jobs (Q5=1) shift weight to longer buckets. High-context work (Q7=5) means fewer quick wins and more integrated effort, so the model assigns more weight to the 1–3 hr and >9 hr buckets. The weights always sum to 1.0 and determine what fraction of your job falls into each duration category. Additionally, the gate softness parameter (which controls how sharply tasks transition from "AI can't do this" to "AI can do this") also varies by seniority and task complexity: entry-level structured work has sharper transitions, while senior complex work has smoother, more gradual automation curves.
  • Readiness $A_{\text{job}}(t)$: $$A_{\text{job}}(t) = \sum_i w_i G_i(H_r(t))$$ This weighted sum tells us what fraction of your job AI can perform at time $t$. If you're an entry-level role with 40% of tasks under 5 minutes and AI's capability $H_r(t)$ has reached 10 minutes, the <5 min gate is fully open ($G_1 \approx 1.0$), contributing $0.40 \times 1.0 = 0.40$ to readiness. Jobs with more long-duration tasks need higher $H_r$ before their readiness $A(t)$ crosses the coverage threshold $\theta$, delaying automation.
  • Coverage bar $\theta$: Baseline $0.50$ shifts based on multiple factors. Data-rich jobs (Q2), standardized workflows (Q6), and fast feedback loops (Q8) lower $\theta$ because automation becomes viable at lower AI capability levels (easier to specify, validate, and deploy). Conversely, tacit knowledge, high context, and compliance-heavy roles push $\theta$ upward before hazard activates. Range: $[0.35, 0.72]$. The hard upper bound of $0.72$ represents the theoretical maximum coverage threshold, though in practice most roles reach a ceiling around $0.68$ with extreme scores on protective factors. This gate controls how much of the job must be automatable before displacement risk activates.
  • User multipliers $M_{\text{user}}(s)$: Questionnaire responses exponentiate into amplifier and friction sums, then are capped between $0.33\times$ and $3\times$ for responsiveness.

How your answers move the curve

Each questionnaire response maps to a coded value $x_i$. All responses use the same coding rule: Likert 1-5 responses convert to the symmetric set $\{-2,-1,0,+1,+2\}$ (i.e., $1\to -2, 3\to 0, 5\to +2$). Effects run in the correct direction via the weight's sign. The model tracks two channels: an amplifier sum $\sum w_{\text{amp}} x_i$ and a friction sum $\sum w_{\text{fric}} x_i$. After clamping each sum to $[-2, 2]$ we exponentiate them, multiply the results, then bound the overall multiplier to $[0.33, 3.0]$ for responsiveness.

The prompts cluster into four themes:

  • AI readiness (Q1-Q4): High scores strengthen the amplifier channel when you indicate strong capability for AI learning and completing your tasks. Additionally, Q1 (current AI performance), Q2 (data availability), Q3 (benchmark clarity), and Q4 (task digitization) all contribute to role explicitness ($s_e$), which lowers the coverage threshold $\theta$ — making automation viable at lower overall capability levels.
  • Task structure (Q5–Q9): These questions affect the blue curve (technical feasibility) through multiple mechanisms: (1) Q5, Q6 shift the task duration profile (structured → shorter buckets, complex → longer buckets), (2) Q2, Q5, Q6, Q8 contribute to $s_e$ which lowers $\theta$ (data‑rich, decomposable, standardized, fast‑feedback jobs become viable at lower capability), and (3) Q7, Q9 increase the domain penalty $p_{\text{domain}}$ (high context, tacit knowledge damp capability) — they do not change the doubling time. Together, these determine when your specific job crosses the automation threshold.
  • Human moat (Q10-Q11): Relationship intensity and physical presence both load the friction side and stretch the METR baseline via the domain penalty, pushing the curve out.
  • Firm and personal context (Q12-Q18): Company levers shape implementation delay. Job performance (Q18) delays displacement for top performers while also helping re-employment. Adaptability (Q17) and transferability (Q16) drive re-employment probability.

Role explicitness ($s_e$) mechanism: Questions Q1, Q2, Q3, Q4, Q5, Q6, and Q8 (positive factors) are averaged together, while Q7 and Q9 (context dependency and tacit knowledge — protective factors) are averaged separately. The formula $s_e = 0.65 \times \text{norm}(s_{\text{pos}}) - 0.35 \times \text{norm}(s_{\text{neg}}) + 0.10$ produces a score between 0 and 1. Higher $s_e$ (more explicit roles) reduces $\theta$ via the shift $\Delta_{s_e} = 0.10 \times (0.5 - s_e)$, meaning AI needs less total job coverage before automation becomes economically viable.

Example: An entry-level customer service rep (seniority=1, Q5=5 structured, Q6=5 standardized, Q7=1 low context) gets task weights roughly [40%, 35%, 20%, 4%, 1%] — most work is quick tasks. A senior software architect (seniority=4, Q5=2 complex, Q6=2 variable, Q7=4 high context) gets [12%, 24%, 28%, 24%, 12%] — heavily weighted toward medium and long tasks. Even if AI capability $H_r(t)$ is identical for both, the architect's readiness $A(t)$ stays lower longer because their job has fewer automatable short tasks. This personalization ensures the model reflects real differences in how jobs are structured.

The model also adjusts the gate steepness $\gamma$ based on task characteristics. Structured work, fast feedback, and standardized tasks sharpen the S-curve; messy, collaborative, or bespoke work flattens it. This captures whether adoption snaps into place once the threshold is crossed or grinds along for years.

Calibration: The model is validated on each page load. Setting Q1, Q4, and Q6 to five yields roughly a $2.5\times$ hazard boost versus neutral answers, while stacking protective responses (Q7, Q9, Q11 high) suppresses the hazard by about $50\%$, delays implementation by $\geq 0.7$ years, and pushes the domain penalty above $~2\times$ so readiness clears METR later.

Implementation delay and the green curve

Key insight: The green curve is not a separate hazard — it's the blue curve shifted right by implementation delay (Δ). The blue curve represents when AI can technically do the job; the green curve represents when organizations actually adopt automation, accounting for regulatory friction, labor protections, integration costs, and trust requirements.

The implementation delay derives from company context and individual leverage. Q12 through Q15 examine adoption appetite, labor cost pressure, labor-market tightness, and infrastructure. Q18 (job performance) helps retain top performers, delaying job loss.

Delay function
$$\text{delay} = \operatorname{clip}(1.75 - 1.25 s_{\text{delay}} + \Delta_{\text{seniority}}, 0.3, 4.0),\quad s_{\text{delay}} \in [-2,2],\; \Delta_{\text{seniority}} \in \{-0.4,-0.1,+0.2,+0.4,+0.6\}$$

Positive scores mean less delay: an early-adopting entry-level role (s_delay = +2, Δ_seniority = -0.4) can reduce the delay to as low as $0.3$ years, while defensive environments with senior leadership (s_delay = -2, Δ_seniority = +0.6) stretch it to $4.0$ years. The seniority shift is applied inside the clip function, so the final delay is always bounded to [0.3, 4.0] years regardless of inputs.

Re-employment odds pull from the adaptability prompts. We start at $60\%$, adjust based on transferable skills (Q16), learning speed (Q17), and job performance (Q18) with a scaling factor of $0.2$ per weighted point, subtract a timing penalty if displacement occurs soon, and then apply the seniority boost from the profile (roughly +0% to +10%). The result is clamped between $10\%$ and $85\%$ — No one is guaranteed, but no one is written off.

Timing penalty (graduated scale): If the median technical feasibility hazard occurs within a shorter timeframe, re-employment becomes more difficult due to market saturation. The penalty is a probability reduction applied as follows: median < 3 years: $-0.30$; 3-7 years: $-0.15$; 7-12 years: $-0.05$; >12 years: $0$ (no penalty). This reflects the difficulty of finding alternative employment when many similar roles are being automated simultaneously.

Reading the outputs

The chart shows two survival complements: blue for technical feasibility and green for realized displacement once friction and delay kick in. Because we integrate $\lambda(s)$ with Simpson's rule, the area under each curve is the cumulative probability that automation has already happened.

  • Median timeline: We use a binary search to find the year where cumulative probability hits $50\%$. That becomes the headline median job-loss metric.
  • Risk by 2030 or 2031: The model calculates years from the current date (when you access the page) to these target years, then evaluates $P_{\text{loss}}$ at that future horizon. For example, accessing the calculator in October 2025 means 2030 is ~4.2 years away. This ensures projections stay relevant as time passes.
  • Personal timeline: We surface the $25\%$, $50\%$, $75\%$, and $90\%$ milestones when they fall within the next $10$, $15$, $18$, and $25$ years respectively so you can see the near-term thresholds.
  • Re-employment label: The probability is bucketed into Good, Moderate, Low, or Very Low for quick scanning, with the precise percentage alongside.

Use the controls to stress-test assumptions. METR's doubling is fixed. Higher industry-specific friction increases the horizon penalty $p_{\text{domain}}$ (not the doubling time), and stricter reliability requirements reduce the effective task horizon. Answers that raise role explicitness $s_e$ or ease the coverage bar $\theta$ pivot curves left, while higher rollout friction $\lambda$ or stubborn implementation drag pushes the green curve out. High scores on Q7–Q12 increase $p_{\text{domain}}$, so even at the METR trend, capability must cover more ground before matching software baselines.

How to work with the model

  1. Start with the preset that feels closest to your role, then tweak one cluster of questions at a time so you can see which dimensions move the hazard.
  2. Stress-test the extremes: set AI readiness to five while leaving friction high, then flip it. The distance between those runs is a map of where to invest your energy.
  3. Pair the re-employment score with the green curve. A fast hazard plus weak re-employment is the trigger for upskilling or a job search; a slow hazard plus strong re-employment buys time for deliberate moves.

Use the weight structure as a checklist: strengthen the protective items you can influence, document the assumptions you cannot, and revisit the calculator as real-world signals shift.
