Model Guide

Let's walk through the hazard model that powers the calculator so you can interpret the curves, pressure-test scenarios, and translate the output into career choices.

Updated October 2025
METR Measuring AI ability to complete long tasks

METR's March 2025 study tracks how long a generalist frontier agent can work autonomously before its success rate drops below a chosen threshold. Their task horizon has doubled roughly every seven months since 2019, giving us a grounded baseline rather than guessing at capability curves.

The calculator uses METR's observed 7-month doubling trend to project AI capability growth over time. We start with a baseline task horizon of 137 minutes (GPT-5's 50th percentile performance as of August 2025) and METR's 80% success horizon of 26.37 minutes. We fit a logistic success curve through those quantiles so the reliability adjustment becomes $f(r) = \exp(-\sigma\,\operatorname{logit}(r))$ with $\sigma \approx -1.19$. At the default 95% production bar the effective horizon contracts to roughly 4.1 minutes (about 33× shorter than the 50th percentile), and a 99% requirement would shrink it to about 0.58 minutes (~235× shorter).

  • Doubling trend: METR's empirical 7-month doubling time drives how fast AI capability improves. Every 7 months, AI can handle 2Ã— longer tasks at the same success rate.
  • Reliability gate: We require 95% success before automation kicks in. Technical capability requires a high bar to outperform human error, though the calculator allows a custom reliability gate if tasks can feasibly be automated at a lower success rate.
  • Task mapping: Your job gets decomposed into task duration buckets (2.5, 15, 60, 180, 540 minutes). Once AI can reliably complete enough of these buckets, the hazard channel opens.
  • Scenario levers: Domain friction and required reliability, plus your questionnaire responses, rescale the METR trend but never overwrite it, so the shape always honors the observed 7‑month doubling cadence.

What the calculator actually simulates

The calculator models job displacement as a survival problem where multiple hazard channels compound over time. A true stacked hazard model should consider all other possibilities for displacement, including macroeconomic forces, firm-level performance, reductions in force (RIFs), non-AI displacement hazards, and personal leverage. This calculator focuses only on AI-related hazards at the role, personal, and firm levels. Consider that it is only a portion of your total displacement risk. This model will update over time with new AI-related data and risk considerations.

Survival relationship
$$P_{\text{loss}}(t) = 1 - \exp\left(-\int_0^t \lambda_{\text{total}}(s)\, ds\right)$$
Stacked hazard model
$$\lambda_{\text{total}}(s) = \lambda_{\text{AI}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{macro}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{firm}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{role}}(s) + \lambda_{\text{personal}}(s)$$

The active AI hazard channel ($\lambda_{\text{AI}}(s)$) generates the blue technical feasibility curve. Your firm and personal questionnaire responses then shift this curve forward in time to create the green implementation curve. The same data also determines your re-employment probability.

  • AI hazard: METR task-length data map capability progress into the blue curve.
  • Adaptability delay: Firm and personal answers shift the AI hazard forward in time to produce the green curve.
  • Re-employment likelihood: Adaptability and transferable skill questions translate into the percentage you see on the dashboard.
AI hazard core
$$\lambda_{\text{AI}}(s) = \frac{h_{\max}}{1 + \exp\!\big(-\gamma (A_{\text{job}}(s) - \theta)\big)} \cdot M_{\text{user}}(s)$$

The hazard turns on once METR readiness clears the coverage bar. The model uses a two-level gating system: first, task-level gates determine which individual tasks AI can handle; second, a job-level hazard gate opens when enough of your job becomes automatable. Here's how it works:

  1. Task-level gates open gradually: As AI capability $H_r(t)$ grows via METR's doubling trend, each task bucket's gate $G_i(H)$ opens. Short tasks automate first (gates open when $H$ exceeds their threshold $L_i$), then medium tasks, then long tasks. Your personalized weights $w_i$ determine how much each gate contributes to overall readiness.
  2. Job readiness accumulates: $A(t) = \sum w_i G_i(H)$ sums up all the open gates, weighted by your job profile. This gives a number between 0 and 1 representing what fraction of your job AI can perform.
  3. Job-level hazard gate activates: When $A(t)$ crosses the coverage threshold $\theta$ (typically 0.50), the hazard function $\lambda_{\text{AI}}(t)$ rapidly increases via the logistic gate. Before this point, hazard is near zero; after, it approaches the maximum $h_{\max} \times M_{\text{user}}$.

This structure means automation doesn't happen all at once. AI steadily masters more of your tasks (task gates opening), but job displacement risk only spikes once AI can handle enough of your role to justify replacement (job gate opening). Jobs with more long-duration tasks take longer to reach this threshold.

  • Task horizon $H_r(t)$: $$H_r(t) = \frac{H_{50,0} \cdot 2^{(t \cdot 12)/D_{\text{eff}}}}{p_{\text{domain}} \cdot f(r)}$$ Where $D_{\text{eff}} = D_{\text{METR}} \times \text{domain friction}$ is the effective doubling time. Domain friction $\in [1.0, 2.0]$ represents industry-specific barriers that slow AI capability transfer (physical work, tacit knowledge, high context). The floor at 1.0 ensures capability cannot grow faster than METR's observed frontier. $f(r)$ is the reliability factor (higher reliability divides H, reducing AI's effective max task length), and $p_{\text{domain}} \in [0.5, 3.0]$ damps capability for tacit or physical work.
  • Task buckets: We mirror METR’s five duration categories — <5 min, 5–15 min, 15–60 min, 1–3 hr, and >3 hr — but calibrate representative thresholds for corporate, production‑grade work at [2.5, 15, 60, 180, 540] min. Each bucket uses a logistic gate $$G_i(H) = \frac{1}{1 + \exp\!\left(-\frac{\ln H - \ln L_i}{s}\right)}$$ where $H$ is AI's current capability (maximum task length it can handle) and $L_i$ is the bucket threshold. When $H \gg L_i$, the gate is fully open (AI masters these tasks); when $H \ll L_i$, the gate is closed (AI can't handle them yet). As METR's doubling trend pushes $H$ higher over time, more gates open.
  • Personalized task weights $w_i$: Your answers to Q5 (task decomposability), Q6 (task standardization), Q7 (context dependency), and your seniority level determine how your job breaks down across the five buckets. Entry-level roles skew toward shorter tasks (quick emails, simple coding); executive roles skew toward longer tasks (strategic planning, integration work). Highly structured jobs (Q5=5) concentrate weight in the short buckets; complex jobs (Q5=1) shift weight to longer buckets. High-context work (Q7=5) means fewer quick wins and more integrated effort, so the model assigns more weight to the 1–3 hr and >3 hr buckets. The weights always sum to 1.0 and determine what fraction of your job falls into each duration category.
  • Readiness $A_{\text{job}}(t)$: $$A_{\text{job}}(t) = \sum_i w_i G_i(H_r(t))$$ This weighted sum tells us what fraction of your job AI can perform at time $t$. If you're an entry-level role with 40% of tasks under 5 minutes and AI's capability $H_r(t)$ has reached 10 minutes, the <5 min gate is fully open ($G_1 \approx 1.0$), contributing $0.40 \times 1.0 = 0.40$ to readiness. Jobs with more long-duration tasks need higher $H_r$ before their readiness $A(t)$ crosses the coverage threshold $\theta$, delaying automation.
  • Coverage bar $\theta$: Baseline $0.50$ shifts based on multiple factors. Data-rich jobs (Q2), standardized workflows (Q6), and fast feedback loops (Q8) lower $\theta$ because automation becomes viable at lower AI capability levels (easier to specify, validate, and deploy). Conversely, tacit knowledge, high context, and compliance-heavy roles push $\theta$ up toward ~$0.68$ before hazard activates. Range: $[0.35, 0.72]$. This gate controls how much of the job must be automatable before displacement risk activates.
  • User multipliers $M_{\text{user}}(s)$: Questionnaire responses exponentiate into amplifier and friction sums, then are capped between $0.33\times$ and $3\times$ for responsiveness.

How your answers move the curve

Each questionnaire response maps to a coded value $x_i$. All responses use the same coding rule: Likert 1-5 responses convert to the symmetric set $\{-2,-1,0,+1,+2\}$ (i.e., $1\to -2, 3\to 0, 5\to +2$). Effects run in the correct direction via the weight's sign. The model tracks two channels: an amplifier sum $\sum w_{\text{amp}} x_i$ and a friction sum $\sum w_{\text{fric}} x_i$. After clamping each sum to $[-2, 2]$ we exponentiate them, multiply the results, then bound the overall multiplier to $[0.33, 3.0]$ for responsiveness.

The prompts cluster into four themes:

  • AI readiness (Q1-Q4): High scores strengthen the amplifier channel when you indicate strong capability for AI learning and completing your tasks.
  • Task structure (Q5–Q9): These questions affect the blue curve (technical feasibility) through multiple mechanisms: (1) Q5, Q6 shift the task duration profile (structured → shorter buckets, complex → longer buckets), (2) Q2, Q6, Q8 lower $\theta$ (data‑rich, standardized, fast‑feedback jobs become viable at lower capability), and (3) Q7, Q9 increase the domain penalty $p_{\text{domain}}$ (high context, tacit knowledge damp capability) — they do not change the doubling time. Together, these determine when your specific job crosses the automation threshold.
  • Human moat (Q10-Q11): Relationship intensity and physical presence both load the friction side and stretch the METR baseline via the domain penalty, pushing the curve out.
  • Firm and personal context (Q12-Q18): Company levers shape implementation delay. Job performance (Q18) delays displacement for top performers while also helping re-employment. Adaptability (Q17) and transferability (Q16) drive re-employment probability.

Example: An entry-level customer service rep (seniority=1, Q5=5 structured, Q6=5 standardized, Q7=1 low context) gets task weights roughly [40%, 35%, 20%, 4%, 1%] — most work is quick tasks. A senior software architect (seniority=4, Q5=2 complex, Q6=2 variable, Q7=4 high context) gets [12%, 24%, 28%, 24%, 12%] — heavily weighted toward medium and long tasks. Even if AI capability $H_r(t)$ is identical for both, the architect's readiness $A(t)$ stays lower longer because their job has fewer automatable short tasks. This personalization ensures the model reflects real differences in how jobs are structured.

The model also adjusts the gate steepness $\gamma$ based on task characteristics. Structured work, fast feedback, and standardized tasks sharpen the S-curve; messy, collaborative, or bespoke work flattens it. This captures whether adoption snaps into place once the threshold is crossed or grinds along for years.

Calibration: The model is validated on each page load. Setting Q1, Q4, and Q6 to five yields roughly a $2.5\times$ hazard boost versus neutral answers, while stacking protective responses (Q7, Q9, Q11 high) suppresses the hazard by about $50\%$, delays implementation by $\geq 0.7$ years, and pushes the domain penalty above $~2\times$ so readiness clears METR later.

Implementation delay and the green curve

The green actual job-loss curve is the same hazard shifted forward by the implementation delay we derive from company context and individual leverage. Q12 through Q15 look at adoption appetite, labor cost pressure, labor-market tightness, and infrastructure. Q18 (job performance) helps retain top performers, delaying job loss.

Delay function
$$\text{delay} = \operatorname{clip}(1.75 - 1.25 s_{\text{delay}}, 0.5, 3.0) + \Delta_{\text{seniority}},\quad s_{\text{delay}} \in [-2,2],\; \Delta_{\text{seniority}} \in \{-0.4,-0.1,+0.2,+0.4,+0.6\}$$

Positive scores mean less delay: an early-adopting firm with high wage pressure can reduce the delay to as low as $0.5$ years, while defensive environments stretch it beyond three. Senior leaders pick up additive shifts (up to +0.6 years) because coordinated change takes longer at the top.

Re-employment odds pull from the adaptability prompts. We start at $60\%$, adjust based on transferable skills (Q16), learning speed (Q17), and job performance (Q18) with a scaling factor of $0.2$ per weighted point, subtract up to $30$ points if the median technical hazard lands within three years, and then apply the seniority boost from the profile (roughly +0% to +10%). The result is clamped between $10\%$ and $85\%$ — No one is guaranteed, but no one is written off.

Reading the outputs

The chart shows two survival complements: blue for technical feasibility and green for realized displacement once friction and delay kick in. Because we integrate $\lambda(s)$ with Simpson's rule, the area under each curve is the cumulative probability that automation has already happened.

  • Median timeline: We use a binary search to find the year where cumulative probability hits $50\%$. That becomes the headline median job-loss metric.
  • Risk by 2030 or 2031: The model evaluates $P_{\text{loss}}$ at $5.35$ and $6.35$ years from $2024.65$, matching the scenario years shown on the card.
  • Personal timeline: We surface the $25\%$, $50\%$, $75\%$, and $90\%$ milestones when they fall within the next $10$, $15$, $18$, and $25$ years respectively so you can see the near-term thresholds.
  • Re-employment label: The probability is bucketed into Good, Moderate, Low, or Very Low for quick scanning, with the precise percentage alongside.

Use the controls to stress-test assumptions. METR’s 7‑month doubling is fixed; higher domain friction stretches the effective doubling time, and stricter reliability requirements reduce the effective task horizon. Answers that raise role explicitness $s_e$ or ease the coverage bar $\theta$ pivot curves left, while higher rollout friction $\lambda$ or stubborn implementation drag pushes the green curve out. High scores on Q7–Q12 also stretch $p_{\text{domain}}$, so even at the METR trend, capability must cover more ground before matching software baselines.

How to work with the model

  1. Start with the preset that feels closest to your role, then tweak one cluster of questions at a time so you can see which dimensions move the hazard.
  2. Stress-test the extremes: set AI readiness to five while leaving friction high, then flip it. The distance between those runs is a map of where to invest your energy.
  3. Pair the re-employment score with the green curve. A fast hazard plus weak re-employment is the trigger for upskilling or a job search; a slow hazard plus strong re-employment buys time for deliberate moves.

Use the weight structure as a checklist: strengthen the protective items you can influence, document the assumptions you cannot, and revisit the calculator as real-world signals shift.
